Well-Being Library

Short, research-informed reads.

Each article below draws on research connected to the Center for Self-Determination Theory, which most of the well-being framework used throughout this toolkit is grounded in. If you have time, consider exploring additional research related to your own field or grade level.

Education Week

Understanding Teacher Burnout

Burnout isn't a personal failing. It's a predictable response to chronic, unaddressed stress. It typically shows up in three ways: emotional exhaustion (feeling used up), depersonalization (emotional distance from students or colleagues), and a reduced sense of accomplishment. Recognizing which of these you're experiencing is the first step toward addressing it, rather than pushing through it.

Simply Psychology

The Three Needs Behind Motivation: Autonomy, Competence, Relatedness

According to Self-Determination Theory, well-being at work depends on three psychological needs being met: autonomy (having choice in how you work), competence (feeling effective at what you do), and relatedness (feeling connected to others). When one of these is consistently frustrated (for example, having little say over your schedule), stress tends to build even if the workload itself hasn't changed.

Next Gen Learning

Why Rest Isn't Selfish

Many educators equate rest with falling behind. But sustained performance requires recovery, physically and emotionally. Treating downtime as optional, rather than necessary, is one of the most common paths to burnout.

Schools That Lead

Recognizing the Early Signs of Emotional Exhaustion

Emotional exhaustion often builds gradually: increased irritability, trouble sleeping, dreading tasks you used to enjoy, or feeling "touched out" by the end of the day. Catching these signs early makes it easier to intervene with small, sustainable changes.

Center for Self-Determination Theory

The Overlooked Half of the Classroom: Teachers' Own Motivation

Motivation research in schools has long focused on students, but teachers bring their own need for autonomy, competence, and connection into the classroom. When administrative control, rigid curricula, or a lack of support undermine those needs, teacher motivation suffers, and research shows that undermining ripples outward to affect students too. Schools that protect teachers' basic psychological needs tend to see benefits for everyone, academically and socially.

Quick Reset Tips

Practical strategies you can use in under five minutes.

Box Breathing (60 seconds)

Inhale for 4 counts, hold for 4, exhale for 4, hold for 4. Repeat four times. This activates your parasympathetic nervous system and can be done at your desk between classes.

Source: Cleveland Clinic

A Boundary Script You Can Reuse

"I want to give this my all, so I should wait until tomorrow to give this the attention it deserves." Having a pre-written line ready makes it easier to protect your time in the moment, without needing to improvise an explanation.

Source: ScienceDaily, Virginia Tech study on after-hours email expectations

The 2-Minute Reset

Set a timer and clear your surface, whether that's your desk, inbox, or to-do list, for two minutes. Short, visible progress can lower your sense of being overwhelmed even when the workload hasn't changed. Think, baby steps.

Source: Harvard Business Review, "The Power of Small Wins"

Step Outside For a Moment

Even 90 seconds of daylight or just a change of scenery can help interrupt a stress spiral. If you can't leave where you are, opening a window or a door helps too.

Source: Frontiers in Psychology, University of Michigan "nature pill" study

Name One Thing You Controlled Today

At the end of the day, identify one decision you had autonomy over, even something small. This can counter the feeling that the whole day happened "to" you.

Source: Center for Self-Determination Theory

Resource Guides

Expandable checklists and templates you can adapt.

Stress Management Strategies Checklist
  • Identify your top 3 recurring stressors this week
  • Choose one Quick Reset Tip to try daily
  • Schedule one non-negotiable block of personal time
  • Identify one person you can talk to about work stress
  • Revisit the self-assessment in two weeks

Informed by NEA, "What's Causing Teacher Burnout?"

Classroom Boundary-Setting Guide
  • Define your "response window" for parent/student emails
  • Communicate the boundary clearly and early in the year
  • Prepare a script for in-the-moment requests outside your capacity
  • Revisit boundaries each grading period, since they can change

Informed by ScienceDaily, Virginia Tech after-hours email study

Summer Self-Care Planning Template
  • List 3 things that will likely restore your energy
  • Block time for at least one per week over the summer
  • Note any recurring summer obligations (summer school, second job) and plan recovery around them
  • Set one intention for how you want to feel by the first day back

Informed by NEA, "Make Educator Well-Being a Priority Now"

Autonomy, Competence, and Relatedness Self-Check
  • Autonomy: Where do I have choice in my role? Where do I feel none?
  • Competence: What is one thing I've gotten better at this year?
  • Relatedness: Who do I feel genuinely connected to at work?

Based on Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)

Resources & Planning

For the days stress feels like too much to manage alone. These resources connect you with real, professional support.

If you need to talk to someone now

These services are free, confidential, and available today. Reaching out is a reasonable step, not a last resort.

  • 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline: call or text 988, or chat at 988lifeline.org. Free, confidential, 24/7 support for any kind of crisis, not only suicide risk.
  • Crisis Text Line: text HOME to 741741 for 24/7 text-based crisis support.
  • SAMHSA National Helpline: call 1-800-662-4357. Free, confidential, 24/7 treatment referral and information for mental health or substance use concerns.
  • NAMI HelpLine: call 1-800-950-6264, Monday to Friday, 10am to 8pm ET, for general mental health information and support.

Numbers verified against 988lifeline.org and the SAMHSA National Helpline page.

Find an ongoing therapist

If you're looking for longer-term support rather than a one-time crisis line, a therapist directory lets you filter by insurance, specialty, and location.

Search therapists on Psychology Today

Educator-specific support

Many districts include an Employee Assistance Program (EAP) in their benefits, often free therapy sessions or referrals. Check with your HR department to see what's available to you.
