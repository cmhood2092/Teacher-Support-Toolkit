Well-Being Library

Short, research-informed reads — no more than a few minutes each.

Education Week

Understanding Teacher Burnout

Burnout isn't a personal failing — it's a predictable response to chronic, unaddressed stress. It typically shows up in three ways: emotional exhaustion (feeling used up), depersonalization (emotional distance from students or colleagues), and a reduced sense of accomplishment. Recognizing which of these you're experiencing is the first step toward addressing it, rather than pushing through it.

Simply Psychology

The Three Needs Behind Motivation: Autonomy, Competence, Relatedness

According to Self-Determination Theory, well-being at work depends on three psychological needs being met: autonomy (having choice in how you work), competence (feeling effective at what you do), and relatedness (feeling connected to others). When one of these is consistently frustrated — for example, having little say over your schedule — stress tends to build even if the workload itself hasn't changed. Naming which need feels unmet can help you target where to focus your energy.

Next Gen Learning

Why Rest Isn't Selfish

Many educators equate rest with falling behind. But sustained performance requires recovery — physically and emotionally. Treating downtime as optional, rather than necessary, is one of the most common paths to burnout. Protecting even small pockets of rest during the summer can change how the next school year starts.

Schools That Lead

Recognizing the Early Signs of Emotional Exhaustion

Emotional exhaustion often builds gradually: increased irritability, trouble sleeping, dreading tasks you used to enjoy, or feeling "touched out" by the end of the day. Catching these signs early — rather than waiting for a breaking point — makes it easier to intervene with small, sustainable changes.

Quick Reset Tips

Practical strategies you can use in under five minutes.

🫁

Box Breathing (60 seconds)

Inhale for 4 counts, hold for 4, exhale for 4, hold for 4. Repeat four times. This activates your parasympathetic nervous system and can be done at your desk between classes.

🗣️

A Boundary Script You Can Reuse

"I want to give this the attention it deserves — I can look at it tomorrow morning." Having a pre-written line ready makes it easier to protect your time in the moment, without needing to improvise an explanation.

🧹

The 2-Minute Reset

Clear one visible surface — your desk, inbox, or to-do list — for two minutes. Small, visible progress can lower the sense of being overwhelmed even when the underlying workload hasn't changed.

🚶

Step Outside Between Periods

Even 90 seconds of daylight and a change of scenery can interrupt a stress spiral. If you can't leave the building, a window or open door works too.

✅

Name One Thing You Controlled Today

At the end of the day, identify one decision you had autonomy over — even something small. This counters the feeling that the whole day happened "to" you.

Resource Guides

Expandable checklists and templates you can adapt.

Stress Management Strategies Checklist
  • Identify your top 3 recurring stressors this week
  • Choose one Quick Reset Tip to try daily
  • Schedule one non-negotiable block of personal time
  • Identify one person you can talk to about work stress
  • Revisit the self-assessment in two weeks
Classroom Boundary-Setting Guide
  • Define your "response window" for parent/student emails
  • Communicate the boundary clearly and early in the year
  • Prepare a script for in-the-moment requests outside your capacity
  • Revisit boundaries each grading period — they can change
Summer Self-Care Planning Template
  • List 3 things that genuinely restore your energy
  • Block time for at least one per week over the summer
  • Note any recurring summer obligations (summer school, second job) and plan recovery around them
  • Set one intention for how you want to feel by the first day back
Autonomy, Competence & Relatedness Self-Check
  • Autonomy: Where do I have choice in my role? Where do I feel none?
  • Competence: What is one thing I've gotten better at this year?
  • Relatedness: Who do I feel genuinely connected to at work?
